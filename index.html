About

Standards win the long game.

Most aggregate operations don't fail because of bad equipment or bad people. They fail because standards erode, ownership blurs, and accountability becomes optional. I've spent 15+ years fixing exactly that. Welder to leadman to foreman to production manager, every step earned on the floor.

My focus is the opposite of erosion: operations where expectations are clear, ownership is shared, and performance is repeatable. The goal is simple: leave operations stronger than I found them, and develop people capable of carrying them even further.

Years in mining, aggregates & heavy industry, welder to manager

How: Strategic employee placement and resolving priority issues in real time, rather than letting them stall the line, kept RBM and ABM high. Downtime dropped by catching defects before they reached the line, keeping work staged so crews didn't have to hunt for it, empowering the team to hit the stop button and fix issues immediately instead of working through them, and staying focused on continuous quality improvement. Engagement rose by listening to what people needed, prioritizing work/life balance, launching a recognition program, and running quarterly check-ins on where people stand.

I write about AI in the workplace because the distance between a boardroom slide deck and what actually works on a plant floor is wide. Most people writing about it have never run a crew.

I have.

I write about leadership because I've lived the lessons, the ones that cost something. Those are the ones worth passing on.

At home you'll find me reading Cialdini, Duhigg, or something disruptive from Whitney Johnson.
