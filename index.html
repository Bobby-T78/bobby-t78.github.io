About

Standards win the long game.

Most aggregate operations don't fail because of bad equipment or bad people. They fail because standards erode, ownership blurs, and accountability becomes optional. I've spent 15+ years fixing exactly that. Welder to leadman to production manager, every step earned on the floor.

That path ran through quarry pits, a heavy equipment assembly line, and aggregate plants across Oklahoma and Texas. My focus is the opposite of erosion: operations where expectations are clear, ownership is shared, and performance is repeatable. The goal is simple: leave operations stronger than I found them, and develop people capable of carrying them even further.

Years in mining, aggregates & heavy industry, welder to manager

How the efficiency numbers held: strategic employee placement and resolving priority issues in real time, instead of letting them stall the line.

How downtime dropped: catching defects before they reached the line, staging work so crews never had to hunt for it, and empowering the team to stop and fix instead of working through problems.

How engagement rose: listening to what people needed, protecting work/life balance, launching a recognition program, and running quarterly check-ins on where people stand.

I write about AI in the workplace because the distance between a boardroom slide deck and what actually works on a plant floor is wide. Most people writing about it have never run a crew.

I have.

I write about leadership because I've lived the lessons, the ones that cost something. Those are the ones worth passing on.

At home you'll find me reading Cialdini, Duhigg, or something disruptive from Whitney Johnson.

If that resonates, let's talk.
